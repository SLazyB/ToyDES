8" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Coil_plastic_and_metal_zippers.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Zippers with common teeth variations: metal teeth (top), coil teeth and plastic teeth.</div></div></div><p>A <b>zipper</b>, <b>zip</b>, <b>fly</b>, or <b>zip fastener</b>, formerly known as a <b>clasp locker</b>, is a commonly used device for binding the edges of an opening of <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">fabric</a> or other flexible material, such as on a garment or a bag.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It is used in <a href="/wiki/Clothing" title="Clothing">clothing</a> (e.g., jackets and jeans), <a href="/wiki/Luggage" class="mw-redirect" title="Luggage">luggage</a> and other bags, <a href="/wiki/Sporting_goods" class="mw-redirect" title="Sporting goods">sporting goods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Camping" title="Camping">camping</a> gear (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Tent" title="Tent">tents</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sleeping_bag" title="Sleeping bag">sleeping bags</a>), and other items.  Zippers come in all different sizes, shapes, and colors. <a href="/wiki/Whitcomb_L._Judson" title="Whitcomb L. Judson">Whitcomb L. Judson</a> was an American inventor from Chicago who invented and constructed a workable zipper.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The method, still in use today, is based on interlocking teeth. Initially, it was titled the “hookless fastener” and was later redesigned to become more reliable.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></p><div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" dir="ltr"><h2>Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" for="toctogglecheckbox"></label></span></div><ul><li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Description"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Description</span></a></li><li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#History"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">History</span></a></li><li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-3"><a href="#Types"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">Types</span></a><ul><li class="toclevel-2 tocsection-

" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-
">&#91;
&#93;</a></sup> carrying from tens to hundreds of specially shaped metal or plastic teeth. These teeth can be either individual or shaped from a continuous coil, and are also referred to as <i>elements</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The slider, operated by hand, moves along the rows of teeth. Inside the slider is a Y-shaped channel that meshes together or separates the opposing rows of teeth, depending on the direction of the slider's movement. The word <i>Zipper</i> is <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeia" title="Onomatopoeia">onomatopoetic</a>, because it was named for the sound the device makes when used, a high-pitched <i>zip.</i></p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Novelty_Zippers.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Novelty_Zippers.jpg/220px-Novelty_Zippers.jpg" width="220" height="175" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Novelty_Zippers.jpg/330px-Novelty_Zippers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Novelty_Zippers.jpg/

0px-Novelty_Zippers.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2622" data-file-height="2089" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Novelty_Zippers.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Examples of special zippers with different tape materials, colors and patterns.</div></div></div><p>In many <a href="/wiki/Jacket" title="Jacket">jackets</a> and similar garments, the opening is closed completely when the slider is at the top end.  Some jackets have double-separating zippers with two sliders on the tape. When the sliders are on opposite ends of the tape then the jacket is closed. If the lower slider is raised then the bottom part of the jacket may be opened to allow more comfortable sitting or bicycling. When both sliders are lowered then the zipper may be totally separated. </p><p>Bags, suitcases and other pieces of <a href="/wiki/Luggage" class="mw-redirect" title="Luggage">luggage</a> also often feature two sliders on the tape: the part of the zipper between them is unfastened. When the two sliders are located next to each other, which can be at any point along the tape, the zipper is fully closed. </p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Two_Way_Open_Ended_Zipper.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Two_Way_Open_Ended_Zipper.jpg/220px-Two_Way_Open_Ended_Zipper.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Two_Way_Open_Ended_Zipper.jpg/330px-Two_Way_Open_Ended_Zipper.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Two_Way_Open_Ended_Zipper.jpg/

0px-Two_Way_Open_Ended_Zipper.jpg 2x" data-file-width="326
" data-file-height="2

8" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Two_Way_Open_Ended_Zipper.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A two-way (double-separating) zipper.</div></div></div><p>Zippers may</p><ul><li>increase or decrease the size of an opening to allow or restrict the passage of objects, as in the <a href="/wiki/Fly_(clothing)" title="Fly (clothing)">fly</a> of trousers or in a <a href="/wiki/Pocket" title="Pocket">pocket</a>.</li><li>join or separate two ends or sides of a single garment, as in the front of a jacket, or on the front, back or side of a dress or skirt to facilitate dressing.</li><li>attach or detach a separable part of the garment to or from another, as in the conversion between trousers and <a href="/wiki/Shorts" title="Shorts">shorts</a> or the connection or disconnection of a <a href="/wiki/Hood_(headgear)" title="Hood (headgear)">hood</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Coat_(clothing)" title="Coat (clothing)">coat</a>.</li><li>attach or detach a small pouch or bag to or from a larger one. One example of this is military rucksacks which have smaller pouches or bags attached on the sides using one or two zippers.</li><li>be used to decorate an item.</li></ul><p>These variations are achieved by sewing one end of the zipper together, sewing both ends together, or allowing both ends of the zipper to fall completely apart.</p><p>A zipper costs relatively little, but if it fails, the garment may be unusable until the zipper is repaired or replaced—which can be quite difficult and expensive. Problems often lie with the zipper slider; when it becomes worn it does not properly align and join the alternating teeth. With separating zippers, the insertion pin may tear loose from the tape; the tape may even disintegrate from use. If a zipper fails, it can either jam (i.e. get stuck) or partially break off.</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zipper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><p>In 1851, <a href="/wiki/Elias_Howe" title="Elias Howe">Elias Howe</a> received a <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a> for an "Automatic, Continuous Clothing Closure". He did not try seriously to market it, missing recognition he might otherwise have received.<sup id="cite_ref-ansun_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ansun-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  Howe's device was more like an elaborate <a href="/wiki/Drawstring" title="Drawstring">drawstring</a> than a true slide fastener.</p><p>Forty-two years later, in 1893 <a href="/wiki/Whitcomb_Judson" class="mw-redirect" title="Whitcomb Judson">Whitcomb Judson</a>, who invented a pneumatic street railway, marketed a "Clasp Locker". The device served as a (more complicated) hook-and-eye shoe fastener. With the support of <a href="/wiki/Businessman" class="mw-redirect" title="Businessman">businessman</a> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Colonel_Lewis_Walker&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Colonel Lewis Walker (page does not exist)">Colonel Lewis Walker</a>, Judson launched the <a href="/wiki/Universal_Fastener_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Universal Fastener Company">Universal Fastener Company</a> to manufacture the new device. The clasp locker had its public debut at the <a href="/wiki/1893_Chicago_World%27s_Fair" class="mw-redirect" title="1893 Chicago World&#39;s Fair">1893 Chicago World's Fair</a> and met with little commercial success.<sup id="cite_ref-ansun_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ansun-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Judson is sometimes given credit as the inventor of the zipper, but he never made a practical device.</p><p>The company, reorganized as the Fastener Manufacturing and Machine Company, moved to Hoboken, N.J. in 1901.  <a href="/wiki/Gideon_Sundback" title="Gideon Sundback">Gideon Sundback</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Swedes" title="Swedes">Swedish</a>-American <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineer" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical engineer">electrical engineer</a>, was hired to work for the company in 1906.  Good technical skills and a marriage to the plant-manager's daughter <a href="/w/index.php?title=Elvira_Aronson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Elvira Aronson (page does not exist)">Elvira Aronson</a> led Sundback to the position of head designer. The company moved to Meadville, PA, where it operated for most of the 20th century under the name <a href="/wiki/Talon_Zipper" title="Talon Zipper">Talon, Inc.</a>  Sundback worked on improving the fastener and in 1909 he registered a patent in Germany<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>. The US-rights to this invention were on the name of the Meadville company (operating as the Hookless Fastener Co.), but Sundback retained non-U.S. rights and used these to set up in subsequent years the Canadian firm Lightning Fastner Co. in St. Catharines, Ont.  Sundback's work with this firm has led to the common misperception that he was Canadian and that the zipper originated in that country.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></p><p>Gideon Sundback increased the number of fastening elements from four per inch (about one every 6.
&#160;mm) to ten or eleven (around every 2.5&#160;mm), introduced two facing rows of teeth that pulled into a single piece by the slider, and increased the opening for the teeth guided by the slider. The patent for the "Separable Fastener" was issued in 1917. Gideon Sundback also created the manufacturing machine for the new device. The "S-L" or "scrapless" machine took a special Y-shaped wire and cut scoops from it, then punched the scoop dimple and nib, and clamped each scoop on a cloth tape to produce a continuous zipper chain. Within the first year of operation, Sundback's machinery was producing a few hundred feet (around 100 meters) of fastener per day.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In March of the same year, Mathieu Burri, a Swiss inventor, improved the design by adding a lock-in system attached to the last teeth, but his version never got into production due to conflicting patents.</p><p>In 1923 during a trip to Europe Sundback sold his European rights to the Swiss <a href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_Othmar_Winterhalter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Martin Othmar Winterhalter (page does not exist)">Martin Othmar Winterhalter</a><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> who improved the design by using ribs and grooves instead of Sundback's joints and jaws<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and started producing with his Riri-company on large scale first in Germany, then in Switzerland.</p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Zipper_animated.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f0/Zipper_animated.gif" width="320" height="320" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="320" data-file-height="320" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption">Zipper slider brings together the two sides</div></div></div><p>The popular North American term <i>zipper</i>, (UK <i>zip</i>, or occasionally <i>zip-fastener</i>), came from the <a href="/wiki/B._F._Goodrich_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="B. F. Goodrich Company">B. F. Goodrich Company</a> in 1923. The company opted  to use Gideon Sundback's fastener on a new type of <a href="/wiki/Rubber" class="mw-redirect" title="Rubber">rubber</a> boots (or <a href="/wiki/Galoshes" title="Galoshes">galoshes</a>) and referred to it as the zipper, and the name stuck. The two chief uses of the zipper in its early years were for closing boots and <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a> pouches. Zippers began being used for clothing in 1925 by Schott NYC on leather jackets.<sup id="cite_ref-ansun_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ansun-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></p><p>In the 1930s, a sales campaign began for children's clothing featuring zippers. The campaign praised zippers for promoting self-reliance in young children by making it possible for them to dress in self-help clothing. The zipper beat the button in 1937 in the "Battle of the Fly", after <a href="/wiki/French_fashion" title="French fashion">French fashion</a> designers raved over zippers in men's trousers. <i><a href="/wiki/Esquire_(magazine)" title="Esquire (magazine)">Esquire</a></i> declared the zipper the "Newest Tailoring Idea for Men" and among the zippered fly's many virtues was that it would exclude "The Possibility of Unintentional and Embarrassing Disarray."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></p><p>The most recent innovation<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> in the zipper's design was the introduction of models that could open on both ends, as on jackets. Today the zipper is by far the most widespread fastener, and is found on clothing, luggage, leather goods, and various other objects.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zipper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Reissverschluss_Helix.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Reissverschluss_Helix.jpg/220px-Reissverschluss_Helix.jpg" width="220" height="296" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Reissverschluss_Helix.jpg/330px-Reissverschluss_Helix.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Reissverschluss_Helix.jpg/

0px-Reissverschluss_Helix.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1002" data-file-height="13
7" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Reissverschluss_Helix.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Coil_zipper" title="Coil zipper">coil zipper</a> with its slider removed.</div></div></div><ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Coil_zipper" title="Coil zipper">Coil zippers</a></b> now form the bulk of sales of zippers worldwide. The slider runs on two coils on each side; the <i>teeth</i> are formed by the windings of the coils. Two basic types of coils are used: one with coils in spiral form, usually with a cord running inside the coils; the other with coils in ladder form, also called the Ruhrmann type. Coil zippers are made of polyester coil and are thus also termed polyester zippers. Nylon was formerly used and though only polyester is used now<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, the type is still also termed a <a href="/wiki/Nylon_zipper" class="mw-redirect" title="Nylon zipper">nylon zipper</a>.</li><li><b>Invisible zippers</b> have the teeth hidden behind a tape, so that the zipper is <i>invisible</i>. It is also called the <i>Concealed</i> zipper. The tape's color matches the garment's, as does the slider's and the puller's. This kind of a zipper is common in <a href="/wiki/Skirt" title="Skirt">skirts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dress" title="Dress">dresses</a>. Invisible zippers are usually coil zippers. They are also seeing increased use by the military and emergency services because the appearance of a button down shirt can be maintained, while providing a quick and easy fastening system. A regular invisible zipper uses a lighter lace-like fabric on the zipper tape, instead of the common heavier woven fabric on other zippers.</li><li><b>Reverse coil zippers</b> are a variation of the coil zipper.  In a reverse coil zipper, the coil is on the reverse (back) side of the zipper and the slider works on the flat side of the zipper (normally the back, now the front). Unlike an invisible zipper where the coil is also on the back, the reverse coil shows stitching on the front side and the slider accommodates a variety of pulls (the invisible zipper requires a small, tear-drop pull due to the small slider attachment). Water resistant zippers are generally configured as reverse coil so that the pvc coating can cover the stitching. A rubber or PVC coated reverse zipper is called a waterproof zipper.</li><li><b><a href="/wiki/Metal_zipper" title="Metal zipper">Metal zippers</a></b>  are the classic zipper type, found mostly in <a href="/wiki/Jeans" title="Jeans">jeans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pencil_case" title="Pencil case">pencil cases</a> today. The teeth are not a coil, but are individual pieces of metal molded into shape and set on the zipper tape at regular intervals. Metal zippers are made in brass, aluminum and nickel, according to the metal used for teeth making. All these zippers are basically made from flat wire. A special type of metal zipper is made from pre-formed wire, usually brass but sometimes other metals too. Only a few companies in the world have the technology. This type of pre-formed metal zippers is mainly used in high grade jeans-wear, work-wear, etc., where high strength is required and zippers need to withstand tough washing.</li><li><b>Plastic-molded zippers</b>  are identical to metallic zippers, except that the teeth are plastic instead of metal. <a href="/wiki/Metal_zipper" title="Metal zipper">Metal zippers</a> can be painted to match the surrounding fabric; plastic zippers can be made in any color of plastic. Plastic zippers mostly use <a href="/wiki/Polyacetal" class="mw-redirect" title="Polyacetal">polyacetal</a> resin, though other <a href="/wiki/Thermoplastic_polymer" class="mw-redirect" title="Thermoplastic polymer">thermoplastic polymers</a> are used as well, such as <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">polyethylene</a>. Used most popularly for pencil cases, small plastic pouches and other useful stationery.</li><li><b>Open-ended zippers</b>  use a <i>box and pin</i> mechanism to lock the two sides of the zipper into place, often in jackets. Open-ended zippers can be of any of the above described types.</li><li><b>Two way open-ended zippers</b>  Instead of having an insertion pin and pin box at the bottom, a two way open-ended zipper has a puller on each end of the zipper tape. Someone wearing a garment with this kind of zipper can slide up the bottom puller to accommodate more leg movement without stressing the pin and box of a one-way open-ended zipper. It is most commonly used on long coats.</li><li><b>Two way closed-ended zippers</b>  are closed at both ends; they are often used in <a href="/wiki/Luggage" class="mw-redirect" title="Luggage">luggage</a> and can have either one or two pullers on the zipper.</li><li><b>Magnetic zippers</b>  allow for one-handed closure and are used in sportswear. <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="https://gizmodo.com/i-have-seen-the-future-and-its-a-one-handed-magnetic-z-1

6
09950">[1]</a></li></ul><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_and_water_tightness">Air and water tightness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zipper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=
" title="Edit section: Air and water tightness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:WP_zip.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/WP_zip.jpg/300px-WP_zip.jpg" width="300" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/WP_zip.jpg/
50px-WP_zip.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/WP_zip.jpg/600px-WP_zip.jpg 2x" data-file-width="102
" data-file-height="563" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:WP_zip.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Waterproof zipper on a diving dry suit. The exterior metal segments clamp the waterproof sheeting over the concealed zipper teeth. The zipper teeth are not visible in this image (obscured by the edges of the waterproof sheet).</div></div></div><p>Airtight zippers were first developed by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> for making high-altitude <a href="/wiki/Pressure_suits" class="mw-redirect" title="Pressure suits">pressure suits</a> and later <a href="/wiki/Space_suits" class="mw-redirect" title="Space suits">space suits</a>, capable of retaining air pressure inside the suit in the <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a> of space.<sup id="cite_ref-1
" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1
">&#91;1
&#93;</a></sup></p><p>The airtight zipper is built like a standard toothed zipper, but with a waterproof sheeting (which is made of fabric-reinforced polyethylene and is bonded to the rest of the suit) wrapped around the outside of each row of zipper teeth. When the zipper is closed, the two facing sides of the plastic sheeting are squeezed tightly against one another (between the C-shaped clips) both above and below the zipper teeth, forming a double seal.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></p><p>This double-mated surface is good at retaining both vacuum and pressure, but the fit must be very tight, to press the surfaces together firmly. Consequently, these zippers are typically very stiff when zipped shut and have minimal flex or stretch. They are hard to open and close because the zipper anvil must bend apart teeth that are being held under tension. They can also be derailed (and damage the sealing surfaces) if the teeth are misaligned while straining to pull the zipper shut.</p><p>These zippers are very common where airtight or watertight seals are needed, such as on <a href="/wiki/Scuba_diving" title="Scuba diving">scuba diving</a> <a href="/wiki/Dry_suit" title="Dry suit">dry suits</a>, ocean <a href="/wiki/Survival_suit" title="Survival suit">survival suits</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hazmat_suit" title="Hazmat suit">hazmat suits</a>.</p><p>A less common water-resistant zipper is similar in construction to a standard toothed zipper, but includes a molded plastic ridge seal similar to the mating surfaces on a <a href="/wiki/Ziploc_bag" class="mw-redirect" title="Ziploc bag">ziploc bag</a>. Such a zipper is easier to open and close than a clipped version, and the slider has a gap above the zipper teeth for separating the ridge seal. This seal is structurally weak against internal pressure, and can be separated by pressure within the sealed container pushing outward on the ridges, which simply flex and spread apart, potentially allowing air or liquid entry through the spread-open ridges. Ridge-sealed zippers are sometimes used on lower-cost surface <a href="/wiki/Dry_suit" title="Dry suit">dry suits</a>.</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anti-slide_zipper_locks">Anti-slide zipper locks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zipper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Anti-slide zipper locks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><p>Some zippers include a designed ability for the slider to hold in a steady open or closed position, resisting forces that would try to move the slider and open the zipper unexpectedly. There are two commons ways this is accomplished:</p><p>The zipper handle can have a short protruding pin stamped into it, which inserts between the zipper teeth through a hole on the slider, when the handle is folded down flat against the zipper teeth. This appears on some brands of trousers. The handle of the fly zipper is folded flat against the teeth when it is not in use, and the handle is held down by both slider hinge tension and the fabric flap over the fly.</p><p>The slider can also have a two-piece hinge assembly attaching the handle to the slider, with the base of the hinge under spring tension and with protruding pins on the bottom that insert between the zipper teeth. To move the zipper, the handle is pulled outward against spring tension, lifting the pins out from between the teeth as the slider moves. When the handle is released the pins automatically engage between the zipper teeth again. They are called "auto-lock sliders".</p><p>A three-piece version of the above uses a tiny pivoting arm held under tension inside the hinge. Pulling on the handle from any direction lifts the pivoting arm's pins out of the zipper teeth so that the slider can move.</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Components">Components</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zipper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Components">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Reissverschluss_Teile_2_fcm.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Reissverschluss_Teile_2_fcm.jpg/210px-Reissverschluss_Teile_2_fcm.jpg" width="210" height="260" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Reissverschluss_Teile_2_fcm.jpg/315px-Reissverschluss_Teile_2_fcm.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Reissverschluss_Teile_2_fcm.jpg/

common gauge sizes are #3, #5, #7, #8 and #10, the bigger the number, the wider the teeth/chain width is.)</li><li>Bottom Stop (A device affixed to the bottom end of a zipper, to prevent further movement of the half of the zipper from separating.)</li><li>Bottom Tape Extension (The fabric part of the zipper, that extends beyond the teeth, at the bottom of the chain.)</li><li>Single Tape Width (Refers to the width of the fabric on one side of the zipper chain.)</li><li>Insertion Pin (A device used on a separating zipper whose function is to allow the joining of the two zipper halves.)</li><li>Retainer Box or Pin Box (A device used on a separating zipper whose function is to correctly align the pin, to begin the joining of the zipper halves.)</li><li>Reinforcement Film (A strip of plastic fused to each half of the zipper tape to allow a manufacturer to electronically "weld" the zipper onto the garment or item that is being manufactured, without the need of sewing or stitching.)<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li></ol><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacturing">Manufacturing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zipper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Manufacturing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><p><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a> reported in 2003 that although the zipper market in the 1960s was dominated by <a href="/wiki/Talon_Zipper" title="Talon Zipper">Talon Zipper</a> (USA) and Optilon (Germany), Japanese manufacturer <a href="/wiki/YKK" title="YKK">YKK</a> grew to become the industry giant by the 1980s. American folklorist <a href="/wiki/Jan_Brunvand" class="mw-redirect" title="Jan Brunvand">Jan Brunvand</a> noted that "The zipper has been the subject of jokes and legends since... the 1920s". Those stories reflect "modern anxieties and desires", emphasizing embarrassments and accidents, primarily involving the flies of men's trousers in stories such as "The Unzipped Stranger" and "The Unzipped Fly".<sup id="cite_ref-McKenna2009_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McKenna2009-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Durability_and_repairs">Durability and repairs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zipper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Durability and repairs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><p>The zipper is often the least durable component in any garment or type of equipment. Most often the zipper fails to close due to a worn or bent slider not being able to apply the necessary force to the sides of the teeth to cause them to interlock. This problem can sometimes be redressed by using small pliers to carefully squeeze the back part of the slider together a fraction of a millimeter. This can compensate for the wear of the slider. The slider is typically made as a <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> <a href="/wiki/Die_casting" title="Die casting">diecast</a> which breaks easily. It is necessary to reduce the force on the pliers before it can be felt that the slider actually gives in. If it is not yet possible to successfully close the zipper, the pressure applied to the slider should only gradually be increased. Another way to reduce the gap of the open end of the slider is by preparing a small block of wood by sawing a slot into one end so that it fits over the upper arm of the slider. Then a hammer can be used to exact a force onto the slider by carefully hitting the wood.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup></p><p>When the protective coating of the diecast slider has been worn off by prolonged usage, the material can corrode. The <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> products are usually metal <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a> which can accumulate and block the slider from moving. When this happens the salt can often be dissolved by submerging the slider in vinegar or another mild acid. Otherwise the slider needs to be removed and replaced.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zipper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><ul><li><a href="/wiki/Funicular" title="Funicular">Funicular</a>—A "zipper train" is a type of funicular train, sometimes called "cremallera" in Spanish</li><li><a href="/wiki/Zipper_storage_bag" title="Zipper storage bag">Zipper storage bag</a></li></ul><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zipper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><ul><li>Henry Petroski: <i>The Evolution of Useful Things</i> (1992); <a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Book_Number" title="International Standard Book Number">ISBN</a>&#160;<a href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/0-679-7
